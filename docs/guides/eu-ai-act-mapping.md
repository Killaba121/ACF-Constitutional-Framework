## 1. Introduction

The European Union Artificial Intelligence Act (EU AI Act) represents the world's first comprehensive legal framework for AI regulation. With enforcement beginning **August 2026**, organizations deploying AI systems in EU jurisdictions face immediate compliance requirements.

This document maps the Autonomous Constitutional Framework's protocols and governance mechanisms to EU AI Act requirements, demonstrating how ACF's internal constitutional substrate provides structural compliance rather than retroactive policy overlays.

### Key Insight

The EU AI Act fundamentally requires that AI systems be **transparent, accountable, and subject to human oversight**. Most compliance approaches achieve this through external monitoring and documentation. ACF achieves it through **architectural design** — these properties are embedded in the system's constitutional substrate rather than layered on after deployment.

## 2. EU AI Act Overview

### Risk Classification Tiers

The EU AI Act categorizes AI systems into four risk tiers:

| Risk Tier | Description | Requirements | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Unacceptable** | Prohibited AI practices | Banned outright | Social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance (with exceptions) |
| **High-Risk** | Systems affecting fundamental rights | Full compliance framework required | Critical infrastructure, employment, education, law enforcement |
| **Limited Risk** | Systems with transparency needs | Transparency obligations | Chatbots, deepfake generators, emotion recognition |
| **Minimal Risk** | Low-risk applications | Voluntary codes of practice | Spam filters, AI-enhanced games |

### Enforcement Timeline

| Date | Milestone |
|---|---|
| **August 2024** | EU AI Act enters into force |
| **February 2025** | Prohibitions on unacceptable-risk AI apply |
| **August 2025** | Rules for general-purpose AI models apply |
| **August 2026** | Full enforcement — all obligations for high-risk AI systems apply |
| **August 2027** | Rules for AI embedded in regulated products apply |

## 5. Limitations and Supplementary Requirements

ACF addresses the **governance architecture** requirements of the EU AI Act. The following areas may require supplementary controls outside the ACF scope:

- **Data processing compliance** — GDPR and data protection requirements may require additional technical controls.
- **Sector-specific requirements** — Certain regulated sectors (medical devices, financial services) have additional requirements beyond the AI Act.
- **Conformity assessment** — Formal conformity assessment procedures (Article 43) require engagement with notified bodies, which is an organizational process external to the framework.
- **Market surveillance** — Ongoing regulatory reporting obligations require organizational processes beyond technical governance.

## 6. Conclusion

The EU AI Act demands that high-risk AI systems be transparent, accountable, robust, and subject to human oversight. ACF's constitutional substrate provides structural compliance with these requirements — not through external policy documents and monitoring dashboards, but through architectural design that makes non-compliance structurally impossible.

For organizations preparing for August 2026 enforcement, ACF offers a path from reactive compliance (adding controls to meet requirements) to proactive compliance (building systems where the requirements are foundational properties).
