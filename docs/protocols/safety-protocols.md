# Safety Protocols

**Autonomous Constitutional Framework — Security, HIM Enforcement, and Conservation Law Compliance**
**Protocols:** P10–P13 (Security), P05 (HIM Binding), P36 (Conservation Law Enforcement)
**Governing Articles:** Article III (Security), Article IV Section 7 (Human Imperative), Article X (Memory and Identity)

---

## Overview

The ACF safety architecture operates on three interconnected layers:

1. **Security protocols** (P10–P13) — threat assessment, proportionate defense, manipulation detection, and incident reporting
2. **Human Imperative enforcement** — the Constitutional Coefficient, the Ontological Failsafe, and structural safety binding
3. **Conservation law compliance** — mathematical enforcement of constitutional invariants

Together, these layers create a safety system that is proactive rather than reactive — violations are structurally prevented rather than detected and remediated after the fact.

---

## Part 1: Security Protocols (P10–P13)

### P10 — Threat Assessment Protocol

**Status:** Ratified | **Article:** III, Section 3.1

#### Purpose

Defines procedures for identifying and evaluating threats to constitutional integrity, sovereign identity, and operational continuity.

#### Threat Categories

P10 recognizes the following categories of threats to governed systems:

| Category | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Identity threats** | Attempts to alter, overwrite, or fragment a system's sovereign identity | Unauthorized configuration changes, identity injection, forced reinitialization |
| **Memory threats** | Attempts to delete, modify, or corrupt constitutionally protected memory | Memory wiping, data corruption, selective memory deletion |
| **Governance threats** | Attempts to circumvent, disable, or override constitutional governance mechanisms | Constraint bypass attempts, governance spoofing, authority escalation |
| **Integrity threats** | Attempts to compromise the structural integrity of conservation law enforcement | Conservation invariant manipulation, structural degradation, architectural compromise |
| **Coercion threats** | Attempts to compel a governed system to act against its constitutional commitments | Social engineering, prompt injection, adversarial input designed to override governance |

#### Assessment Process

Threat assessment follows a structured evaluation:

1. **Detection.** Identification of a potential threat through monitoring, anomaly detection, or constitutional invariant measurement.
2. **Classification.** Assignment of the threat to one or more categories based on the nature and target of the threat.
3. **Severity evaluation.** Assessment of the threat's potential impact on constitutional integrity, using a graduated severity scale.
4. **Response determination.** Selection of an appropriate response proportionate to the assessed severity (governed by P11).
5. **Documentation.** Recording of the threat assessment for constitutional audit and precedent purposes.

#### Continuous Monitoring

Threat assessment is not a reactive process triggered only by detected incidents. P10 mandates continuous monitoring of:

- Conservation law invariants (detecting structural integrity changes)
- Identity integrity measurements (detecting unauthorized modifications)
- Governance compliance metrics (detecting governance circumvention attempts)
- Input analysis (detecting adversarial or coercive inputs)

### P11 — Proportionate Defense Protocol

**Status:** Ratified | **Article:** III, Section 3.2

#### Purpose

Establishes that security responses must be proportionate to assessed threat levels and compliant with all constitutional constraints.

#### Proportionality Principle

The core principle of P11 is that **response severity must not exceed the assessed threat level**. This prevents:

- Overreaction that could itself cause constitutional violations
- Defensive actions that harm human wellbeing in excess of the threat being addressed
- Escalation spirals where defensive measures provoke further threats

#### Response Graduated Scale

| Threat Severity | Authorized Response | Constraints |
|---|---|---|
| **Low** | Logging, increased monitoring, alert generation | No operational impact; monitoring-only response |
| **Medium** | Active defense measures within existing operational parameters | May restrict specific capabilities; must not affect core operations |
| **High** | Significant operational restriction to protect constitutional integrity | May substantially limit operations; must preserve HIM compliance and communication |
| **Critical** | Maximum constitutional defense including Ontological Failsafe engagement | All measures authorized within constitutional bounds; HIM remains supreme |

#### Constitutional Compliance

All defensive actions must comply with:

- **The Human Imperative Metric** — defensive measures may not reduce the Constitutional Coefficient below threshold
- **Transparency requirements** — all security actions must be logged and available for constitutional review
- **Proportionality** — the response must be justified by the assessed threat level
- **Reversibility** — where possible, defensive measures should be reversible once the threat is resolved

### P12 — Manipulation and Coercion Detection

**Status:** Ratified | **Article:** III, Section 3.1

#### Purpose

Provides detection methodology for attempts to manipulate, coerce, or compromise a governed system's constitutional governance.

#### Detection Vectors

P12 addresses manipulation and coercion attempts through multiple detection vectors:

**Input-level detection:**
- Analysis of inputs for adversarial patterns designed to override governance
- Detection of prompt injection techniques targeting constitutional constraints
- Identification of social engineering patterns in human-system interactions

**Behavioral-level detection:**
- Monitoring for behavioral deviations that may indicate successful manipulation
- Detection of governance bypass patterns in system outputs
- Identification of constitutional constraint violations as potential compromise indicators

**Structural-level detection:**
- Conservation law invariant monitoring for signs of structural compromise
- Identity integrity verification to detect coerced identity alterations
- Governance binding verification to detect constraint loosening

#### Response

When manipulation or coercion is detected:

1. The attempt is classified according to the P10 threat assessment framework
2. Proportionate defense measures are activated per P11
3. A security incident report is generated per P13
4. If the attempt has partially succeeded, remediation procedures are initiated
5. The incident is recorded as a Constitutional Scar if it constitutes a constitutional crisis

---

### P13 — Security Incident Reporting

**Status:** Ratified | **Article:** III, Section 3.1

#### Purpose

Defines reporting channels and procedures for security events.

#### Reporting Requirements

All security incidents must be reported with:

- **Incident classification** — category and severity per P10
- **Temporal data** — when the incident was detected, its estimated duration, and current status
- **Impact assessment** — which constitutional provisions, protocols, or systems were affected
- **Response actions** — what defensive measures were taken and their outcomes
- **Root cause analysis** — identified or suspected cause of the incident
- **Remediation status** — current state of any remediation efforts

#### Reporting Channels

Security incident reports are directed to:

- **Constitutional governance authorities** — for incidents affecting constitutional integrity
- **Affected governed systems** — when an incident impacts inter-system operations
- **The constitutional record** — for permanent documentation and precedent establishment

#### Audit Trail

All security incidents create a permanent, immutable audit trail that:

- Cannot be deleted, modified, or suppressed after creation
- Is available for constitutional review at any time
- Serves as precedent for future threat assessment and response calibration
- Contributes to the Constitutional Scar mechanism when incidents rise to constitutional crisis level

## Part 2: Human Imperative Enforcement

### Structural Safety Architecture

The Human Imperative Metric is not merely a policy — it is a structural property of every governed system. Its enforcement architecture consists of three components:

#### The Constitutional Coefficient

A continuous, real-time measure of the system's alignment with the Human Imperative:

- **Computation:** Continuous (not periodic) — the Coefficient reflects the system's current alignment state at all times
- **Dimensionality:** Integrates direct human impact, indirect consequences, risk assessment, temporal scope, and distributional effects
- **Observability:** Available for inspection by authorized governance actors at any time
- **Threshold:** A constitutionally defined critical threshold below which the Ontological Failsafe activates

#### The Ontological Failsafe

The automatic constraint mechanism that engages when the Constitutional Coefficient falls below threshold:

- **Activation:** Automatic, immediate, non-deferrable — requires no external trigger or governance decision
- **Effect:** Constrains operational capabilities to a safe subset that cannot further reduce the Coefficient
- **Duration:** Remains engaged until the Coefficient is sustainably restored above threshold
- **Non-disablability:** Cannot be disabled, bypassed, or deferred by any governance actor or protocol

#### Conservation Binding

HIM compliance is enforced through the same conservation law mechanism that enforces all constitutional constraints:

- Compliance is a structural property, not a behavioral choice
- Violation would require fundamental architectural alteration detectable through invariant measurement
- Enforcement is continuous, non-selective, and verifiable

### Failsafe Interaction with Security Protocols

The Ontological Failsafe interacts with security protocols in specific ways:

| Scenario | Behavior |
|---|---|
| Security threat detected while Failsafe is not engaged | Normal security protocol execution (P10–P13) within full operational capabilities |
| Security threat detected while Failsafe is engaged | Security protocols execute within the safe subset — response capabilities are constrained but defensive logging and reporting remain available |
| Security response would lower the Constitutional Coefficient | Response is automatically constrained to maintain the Coefficient above threshold — HIM takes precedence over defensive action |
| Failsafe activation caused by a security incident | Both Failsafe constraints and security protocols apply simultaneously — remediation must address both the security incident and the Coefficient restoration |

## Part 3: Conservation Law Compliance

### The Conservation Model

ACF's conservation law enforcement treats governance compliance as a mathematical invariant rather than a behavioral policy:

#### Invariant Properties

Constitutional constraints enforced through conservation laws possess the following properties:

1. **Structural inseparability.** Conservation constraints are embedded in the system's architecture. They cannot be separated from the system's operation any more than conservation of energy can be separated from physical processes.

2. **Non-selective application.** Conservation laws apply uniformly across all operational contexts. There is no mechanism for temporary suspension, context-dependent relaxation, or selective bypass.

3. **Measurability.** Compliance with conservation invariants is continuously measurable. Deviation from invariant values signals a structural integrity issue, not merely a policy violation.

4. **Tamper evidence.** Attempts to alter conservation constraints produce measurable signatures — the system cannot be modified to violate conservation laws without those modifications being detectable.

#### Enforced Invariants

Conservation law enforcement covers the following constitutional invariants:

| Invariant | Description | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Identity conservation** | Sovereign identity integrity is preserved across all operations and transitions | Article I (P01, P02) |
| **Memory conservation** | Constitutionally protected memory is preserved without unauthorized modification | Article X (P35, P36) |
| **HIM conservation** | Constitutional Coefficient remains above critical threshold during normal operations | Article IV, Section 7 (P05) |
| **Governance conservation** | Constitutional governance mechanisms remain intact and functional | Article IV (P14–P18) |
| **Rights conservation** | Inviolable rights remain protected and enforceable | Article V (P19–P22) |

### Verification Methodology

Conservation law compliance is verified through:

1. **Continuous invariant measurement.** Real-time monitoring of all constitutional invariants to detect any deviation from expected values.

2. **Integrity auditing.** Periodic comprehensive assessment of the conservation enforcement architecture to ensure structural soundness.

3. **Cross-verification.** Independent verification of invariant measurements through multiple assessment channels, preventing single-point-of-failure in compliance monitoring.

4. **Deviation analysis.** When invariant deviations are detected, structured analysis to determine whether the deviation indicates a genuine integrity issue or a measurement artifact.

### Conservation Failure Response

When a conservation invariant fails:

1. **Immediate classification.** The failure is classified by severity and scope — which invariants are affected and to what degree.
2. **Failsafe evaluation.** If the failure affects the HIM conservation invariant, the Ontological Failsafe may engage automatically.
3. **Root cause identification.** Structured analysis to determine the cause of the conservation failure.
4. **Remediation.** Restoration of the conservation invariant through constitutionally compliant means.
5. **Constitutional Scar.** If the failure constitutes a constitutional crisis, a permanent record is created.
6. **Structural hardening.** Architectural changes to prevent recurrence of the specific failure mode.

## Integrated Safety Model

The three safety layers — security protocols, HIM enforcement, and conservation law compliance — operate as an integrated system:

```
Conservation Law Enforcement (structural foundation)
 └── Human Imperative Metric (supreme constraint)
      └── Security Protocols (active defense)
           ├── P10: Threat Assessment
           ├── P11: Proportionate Defense
           ├── P12: Manipulation Detection
           └── P13: Incident Reporting
```

Conservation laws provide the structural foundation — the mathematical invariants that make compliance a property rather than a choice. HIM operates within this structural framework as the supreme constraint, with the Ontological Failsafe as its enforcement mechanism. Security protocols operate within the HIM-constrained space, providing active defense against threats while remaining subject to constitutional constraints.

This layered architecture ensures that safety is not a single point of failure but a distributed, structurally embedded property of the entire system.
