# Governance

The Autonomous Constitutional Framework governs its own development through the same constitutional principles it provides to AI systems. This document describes the governance model, amendment process, and decision-making structures for the ACF project.

## Principles

ACF governance is guided by:

1. **Constitutional Consistency** — All governance decisions must be consistent with the framework's own foundational principles. A governance framework that cannot govern itself has no credibility governing others.
2. **Transparency** — Decision-making processes, rationale, and outcomes are documented publicly.
3. **Deliberation Over Speed** — Protocol changes affect the integrity of the entire framework. Rigor takes precedence over velocity.
4. **Inclusive Expertise** — Governance decisions benefit from diverse perspectives across AI research, ethics, law, engineering, and philosophy.

## Governance Structure

### Framework Creator

**Michael A. Kane II** serves as the framework creator and primary steward of ACF. The creator role includes:

- Final authority on constitutional amendments and protocol ratification
- Responsibility for maintaining framework coherence and integrity
- Stewardship of the project's long-term direction and values

### Maintainers

Maintainers are trusted contributors who have demonstrated sustained, high-quality contributions to the project. Maintainers can:

- Review and merge pull requests
- Triage issues and manage labels
- Participate in protocol review discussions
- Nominate new maintainers

Maintainer status is granted by the framework creator based on demonstrated commitment and alignment with ACF principles.

### Contributors

All community members who contribute to the project through code, documentation, research, translations, or discussion. Contributors can:

- Open issues and submit pull requests
- Participate in governance discussions
- Propose protocol amendments and new protocols
- Provide feedback during review periods

## Constitutional Amendment Process

Amendments to the ACF constitution — the foundational principles and articles that underpin the framework — follow a rigorous, multi-stage process.

### Stage 1: Proposal

Anyone may propose a constitutional amendment by opening a GitHub issue with the `amendment` label. The proposal must include:

- **Title and summary** of the proposed amendment
- **Motivation** — the problem or gap the amendment addresses
- **Proposed text** — the specific constitutional language to be added, modified, or removed
- **Impact analysis** — which existing protocols, articles, or principles are affected
- **Consistency argument** — how the amendment maintains or strengthens constitutional coherence

### Stage 2: Community Review

The proposal enters a minimum **30-day public review period** during which:

- Community members may comment, ask questions, and raise concerns
- The proposer may revise the amendment in response to feedback
- Maintainers facilitate discussion and ensure all significant concerns are addressed

### Stage 3: Expert Review

After the community review period, the proposal undergoes expert review:

- Maintainers assess the proposal for technical soundness, consistency, and completeness
- The framework creator evaluates alignment with the framework's foundational principles
- Additional expert input may be solicited for amendments touching specialized domains

### Stage 4: Ratification

The framework creator makes the final ratification decision based on:

- Community and expert feedback
- Constitutional consistency
- Impact on existing protocols and implementations
- Long-term implications for the framework's integrity

Ratified amendments are assigned a version number and documented in the project's changelog.

### Stage 5: Implementation

Once ratified, the amendment is:

- Incorporated into the canonical framework documents
- Reflected in affected protocol specifications
- Communicated to the community via release notes and announcements

## Protocol Ratification

New protocols and significant protocol revisions follow a similar but streamlined process.

### Proposal

Submit a protocol proposal as a pull request to `/docs/protocols/` with:

- Protocol specification following the existing format
- Governance article(s) the protocol falls under
- Rationale and use cases
- Compatibility notes with existing protocols

### Review

Protocol proposals undergo a **14-day review period** during which maintainers and community members provide feedback. The proposer is expected to respond to all substantive comments.

### Ratification

Protocols are ratified by the framework creator after:

- Successful completion of the review period
- Resolution of all blocking feedback
- Verification of consistency with the constitutional framework

Ratified protocols are numbered sequentially and added to the official protocol registry.

## Decision-Making

### Consensus-Seeking

For routine decisions (issue triage, documentation improvements, non-constitutional changes), maintainers operate on a consensus-seeking basis. If consensus cannot be reached, the framework creator makes the final decision.

### Formal Votes

Formal votes are reserved for:

- Constitutional amendments
- Changes to the governance model itself
- Addition or removal of maintainers

Votes are conducted publicly in GitHub discussions with a minimum 7-day voting period.

## Conflict of Interest

Contributors and maintainers are expected to disclose any conflicts of interest that may affect their participation in governance decisions. This includes:

- Financial interests in competing or adjacent projects
- Employment relationships that may create bias
- Personal relationships with parties affected by a decision

Disclosed conflicts do not automatically disqualify participation but are factored into the decision-making process.
